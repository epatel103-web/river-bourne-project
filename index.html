🌊 River Bourne Project

St George's Weybridge × South East Rivers Trust

Overview Importance Student Experience Restoration Phases Monitoring Team

🌍 Overview

The River Bourne Project is a long-term environmental restoration initiative based at St George’s Weybridge in partnership with the South East Rivers Trust. It focuses on improving the ecological health of the River Bourne, a tributary of the River Thames system.

The river has historically been impacted by channel straightening, dredging and urban runoff, leading to reduced biodiversity and degraded habitats. This project aims to reverse those impacts through evidence-based restoration.

Students play an active role in real ecological research, contributing to ongoing conservation decisions that directly affect the river environment.

🌱 Why This Project Matters

Healthy rivers are essential ecosystems that support biodiversity, regulate water quality and reduce flood risk. The River Bourne acts as a vital wildlife corridor, connecting habitats across Surrey.

Restoration improves oxygen levels, sediment balance and habitat diversity, allowing species such as brown trout, aquatic insects, kingfishers and herons to return and thrive.

For the school, the project demonstrates long-term environmental responsibility and a commitment to sustainability beyond the classroom. It also gives students the opportunity to directly contribute to local ecological recovery.

🎓 Student Learning & Experience

Students participate in authentic field-based scientific research, acting as environmental scientists working on a live river system. This moves learning beyond theory into real ecological investigation.

They gather, process and interpret environmental data to understand how river systems respond to human impact and restoration strategies.

  • Water quality testing (pH, nitrates, turbidity, dissolved oxygen)
  • Habitat classification and ecological surveying
  • River flow and channel morphology studies
  • Species identification and biodiversity tracking
  • GIS-style mapping and site surveying
  • Long-term environmental data analysis

The project strengthens critical thinking, teamwork, leadership and scientific communication. It also introduces students to careers in ecology, hydrology, conservation science, environmental engineering and sustainability planning.

🛠️ Restoration Phases

Phase One (2018) – Initial Restoration

Early work focused on reversing historical modifications to the river channel. Straightened sections were re-naturalised to improve flow diversity and habitat complexity.

Key outcomes included improved spawning grounds, increased gravel retention and the creation of refuge zones for fish during high flow events.

Phase Two (2022–2024) – Habitat Engineering

This phase introduced targeted river engineering features designed to restore natural processes.

  • Installation of deflectors to redirect flow
  • Construction of berms to create varied habitats
  • Creation of V-deflectors for sediment movement
  • Backwater habitat development from former drainage channels
  • Tree thinning to increase light penetration and biodiversity

These interventions significantly improved habitat diversity and ecological resilience.

Phase Three (2025–2026+) – Student-Led Monitoring

The current phase focuses on long-term ecological monitoring and student involvement in data collection.

  • Student-led field surveys and data collection
  • Design planning and environmental permissions
  • Construction monitoring and impact assessment
  • Long-term biodiversity and water quality tracking

📊 Long-Term Monitoring

A key part of the River Bourne Project is continuous monitoring. Rivers are dynamic systems, meaning conditions change seasonally and over time.

Students revisit the same sites to track ecological change, compare datasets and identify long-term trends in water quality and biodiversity.

This ongoing research helps determine the success of restoration efforts and provides valuable scientific evidence for future river management strategies.
